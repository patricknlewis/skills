# Write a project summary

This skill produces *project summaries* - the single-page writeups on a designer's portfolio that document one product. A summary is **not** a case study (a longer, section-by-section walkthrough of design work where images carry the weight) and **not** a blog post (a narrative, anecdote-led essay). It sits between them: short enough to skim, substantive enough to show judgment.

A project summary answers four questions for someone skimming a portfolio: what is this product, why does it exist, what choices shaped it, and what did building it teach the person whose portfolio this is. It does that in 500-900 words.

## What the skill needs first

A summary is only as good as its source material, and the source material has to come from the designer - their words, their decisions, their surprises. Before drafting, check that the conversation contains:

1. **What the product is and who it's for**, in the designer's own words.
2. **Their actual role**, and who else built it.
3. **Two or three decisions that could have gone another way**, with why they went the way they did.
4. **At least one thing that happened that they didn't expect** - something shipped and pulled, a use case nobody designed for, a result that surprised them.
5. **Product status** (shipped / in development) and the link, if one exists.

Items 3 and 4 feed the two sections readers care about most, and they're the two sections that can never be drafted from inference. If either is missing, do not write a thin version of those sections - switch to interview mode.

## Two modes

**Interview mode.** When source material is missing or thin, generate questions for the designer to answer before any drafting happens. The designer can answer however they like - many record a voice memo or a meeting-notes transcript while talking through the project, which tends to produce better raw material than typing. Tailor the questions to what's missing, drawing from this bank:

- Describe the product to me like I've never seen it. Who is it for?
- What did you actually do on this project? Who else built it, and what did they own?
- What's a decision on this project that could have gone another way? What tipped it?
- What did you try that didn't work? What did you do about it?
- What happened that you didn't plan for - a use case, a result, a way people used it?
- Did anything about pricing, naming, or scope cause a real argument or a real rethink?
- What's the status today, and where can someone see it?

Six to eight questions, max - enough to fill the gaps, not an interrogation. Then stop and wait for the answers. Do not draft "in the meantime."

**Drafting mode.** When the material covers the five requirements, write the summary following the format below. Everything in the draft must trace back to the designer's input; the format supplies the shape, never the substance.

## Who's reading

Hiring managers, founders, and potential clients - people evaluating the designer, skimming many projects in one sitting. Each summary has to stand alone, establish credibility fast, and demonstrate *how the designer thinks*, with the product as evidence.

That last point is the whole game. A summary that only describes the product is a press release. A summary that shows the decisions behind the product is a portfolio piece.

## Structure

Every summary follows this shape:

1. **Opening block** (no header). What the product is, why it exists, who built it, and the writer's role. 1-3 short paragraphs.
2. **Features** - what the product does, walked through as a user experiences it.
3. **Underlying thesis** - the bet underneath the product. Optional: include it when the product has a real strategic argument, cut it when it doesn't.
4. **Strategic decisions** - bulleted. Each bullet is a decision with its reasoning, not a feature.
5. **Learnings** - what building it actually taught the writer. For products still in development, use **Status** instead, and include an invitation (beta testers, waitlist, feedback).
6. **Closing line + link** - one sentence pointing at the live product, then a product image.

### Section heading rules

- Sentence case, one or two words: `Features`, `Underlying thesis`, `Strategic decisions`, `Learnings`, `Status`.
- A summary may earn one custom section when the story demands it - a pivot, an acquisition, a rebuild. One per summary, max, and only when the content genuinely doesn't fit the standard sections.
- **Never a Traction section.** Numbers live inside the narrative where they support a point - an open rate inside the decision that produced it, a usage stat inside the learning it taught. A standalone metrics section reads like a pitch deck, and a portfolio is not a pitch deck.

## The opening block

The opening block does a lot of work in very little space. Its parts, in rough order:

1. **Product definition.** Plain language, concrete. A stranger should know what the product is by the end of sentence one. If sentence one needs the category explained first, the sentence isn't done.
2. **Why it exists.** The origin in miniature: the frustration, the personal stake, the observed gap. This is where personality is allowed in. "We got tired of paying for [incumbent]" is a better origin than "we identified an opportunity in the market."
3. **Who built it and the writer's role.** Collaborators get named and credited. Company or studio context gets one clause.

### Role sentence rules

Every summary states the writer's role, and **the constructions must genuinely vary across the portfolio** - different grammatical shapes, not the same sentence with swapped synonyms. Some shapes to rotate through:

- A standalone sentence: "I led product, design, and a good part of the front-end."
- Folded into the company context: "I was the first designer at [company], where I led design and wrote much of the front-end."
- A trailing clause: "…with me leading design and contributing to product strategy."
- Verb-led variation: "I designed it, drove the product, and wrote the code that runs it."

Before writing a new role sentence, check the constructions already used elsewhere in the portfolio and write one with a different shape. The test: if two summaries' role sentences could be swapped without anyone noticing, rewrite one.

Titles should match what the person actually did, not the most senior-sounding label. "Founding designer" when there were no reports beats "Head of Design." Inflated titles read as inflated everywhere else too.

## Features section

Written as a walkthrough, not a list. Describe the product the way a user moves through it: "You upload a photo, pick a style, and…" Second person, present tense, concrete verbs.

Two or three paragraphs, max. If the product has two distinct halves - a builder-facing side and a viewer-facing side, a capture flow and a feed - give each its own paragraph.

Resist the urge to enumerate every feature. Pick the ones that carry the product's identity and let the rest go. A summary that lists twelve features says less than one that explains three.

## Underlying thesis section

The "why this product deserves to exist" argument. The strongest versions:

- Name the market pattern plainly: what does every incumbent in the category do the same way?
- State the bet as a bet: "This is a bet that [behavior] becomes [norm]."
- Ground it in the writer's own experience where possible: "We learned this the hard way shipping [previous thing]."

One to three paragraphs. If the thesis can't fill a paragraph without padding, the product doesn't need this section - cut it rather than inflate it. A utility doesn't need a worldview.

## Strategic decisions section

The heart of the summary, and the section evaluators read most closely. Rules:

- **Bulleted, with a bolded short label** ending in a period, followed by the reasoning: "**Flat rate, not per seat.** One price for the whole team, because…"
- Each bullet is a *decision* - something that could have gone another way - with the reasoning that tipped it. A feature description is not a decision.
- 3-4 bullets. More than four dilutes.
- The best bullets name what was given up: "It would have been easy to do X. We did Y instead, because…" The road not taken is what proves a decision was made.
- Good categories for this slot: pricing decisions (shows business thinking), naming decisions (shows craft), trust and privacy decisions (shows user empathy), scoping decisions (shows restraint). A portfolio full of summaries where every decision is visual is a portfolio that undersells the writer.

## Learnings section

The most fabrication-prone section, so the strictest rules live here:

- **Learnings must come from what actually happened.** Source them from the designer's input - the interview answers, a transcript, their notes. If the material is thin, drop back to interview mode and ask; never invent a plausible-sounding lesson. Readers who've built things can smell a synthetic learning instantly.
- The strongest learnings are specific and surprising: the use case nobody designed for, the feature the data killed, the thing dogfooding revealed. "We learned the importance of user feedback" is not a learning. "We shipped badges, watched nobody open them, and pulled them out" is.
- One or two learnings, told as short stories with their evidence. Not a numbered list of takeaways.
- A learning that connects back to an earlier company or project is a feature, not a digression - cross-referencing shows a career, not just a project.

### Status section (for in-development products)

Replaces Learnings when the product hasn't shipped or is in active early development. Two parts: a one-sentence honest status, and an invitation - beta testers, a waitlist, a request for feedback. Honest "still building" beats vague "coming soon."

## Closing

One sentence with the link: "You can see [Product] at [url]" or "[Product] is on the App Store." For products with no link yet, a closing observation works - one concrete line about what the project was, not a reflection on the journey.

Then the product image. No recap paragraph, no personal epilogue, no "I'm excited about what's next."

## Voice and formatting

The register is confident and factual with personality at the edges - these are portfolio pieces, not personal essays, so the anecdotes live in the opening and the learnings, not throughout.

- Plain words. "Use" beats "leverage." "Help" beats "facilitate."
- Sentence case headers.
- Italics for emphasis (rare), bold only for headers and decision labels.
- No correlative constructions ("isn't just X, it's Y").
- No theatrical pivots, foreshadowing beats, or section-turn drumrolls.
- No aphoristic closes. The last line should be concrete, not a fortune cookie.
- Don't label a decision "strategic" and stop there - the supporting specifics *are* the demonstration. If the specifics aren't available, neither is the claim.
- Numbers stated plainly, with honest hedges where they exist ("close to," "around"). A hedged real number beats a confident round one.
- Collaborators credited by name. Solo credit for team work reads badly to exactly the people a portfolio is for.

## Fabrication guard

This is the enforcement of "What the skill needs first." The intake gate keeps invented content out at the front; this check catches anything that slipped through during drafting. Before finishing any draft:

1. Is every metric, date, and name something the designer actually provided? If not, remove it or ask - never round a vague memory into a confident number.
2. Is every learning traceable to the designer's input? If a Learnings section is running on inference, that means intake was incomplete - drop back to interview mode rather than patching the draft.
3. Is the product's status accurate - shipped vs. in development vs. explored-but-rejected? Don't describe an explored feature as shipped.
4. Does the role sentence repeat a construction already used elsewhere in the portfolio? If yes, reshape it.
5. Does a Traction section exist? Delete it and move the numbers into the narrative.

The failure mode this guards against is specific: a draft that reads well and is quietly wrong. A designer who publishes an invented learning and gets asked about it in an interview is worse off than one with no summary at all.

## A test for any draft

1. Could a stranger say what the product is after sentence one?
2. Does each Strategic decisions bullet contain a real fork in the road, with reasoning?
3. Would the Learnings section survive the portfolio owner asking "did I actually tell you that?"
4. Read the role sentence next to the portfolio's other role sentences. Does it have its own shape?
5. Does it end on the link or a concrete line, not a reflection?
