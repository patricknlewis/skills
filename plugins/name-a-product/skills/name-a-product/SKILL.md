# Name a product

This skill helps name a product the way a designer who's named things well actually does it: start from the job the product does, find a real thing that does that job, and check what people *hear* when you say it out loud. It does the divergent work and the convergent work, shows its reasoning, and hands the decision back to the designer.

It is for **product names** - apps, companies, tools. Not feature names. A feature name has to live inside an existing product's established voice, which is a different and more constrained problem; this skill assumes the name gets to set its own world.

## What this skill does not do

It does not pick the winner for you, and it does not draw or generate anything visual.

- **No single-name verdict.** Naming is too dependent on context the skill doesn't have - the rest of your brand, the names already in your space, the thing you privately love. The skill narrows to a shortlist and names the tradeoffs. The choice stays yours.
- **No logos, no marks, no generated images.** When a name carries a visual possibility, the skill surfaces the raw material - the real-world referent, what it's associated with, the forms it has historically taken - and stops there. It will say "Concordia was a Roman goddess of harmony, often shown in profile on old coins." It will not draw the coin. Seeing the mark, and making it, is the designer's job.

This is the same principle in both cases: do the research and the divergent thinking, leave the creative judgment to the person who has the context.

## Intake

Get the brief before generating anything. Names made without a brief sound nice and mean nothing. Ask for:

1. **What's the product, and what's the job it does?** Push for the job as a verb if possible - "it coordinates a team's tasks," "it tries clothes on you," "it markets your product on camera." The job is the single most useful input, because the strongest method works directly from it.
2. **Is there a brand world it lives in?** A theme, a metaphor the company already runs on, an existing family of product names. (A pirate-themed company names differently than a clinical-sounding health company.)
3. **What should it feel like?** Calm, playful, serious, fast, premium, friendly.
4. **Category convention - sit inside it or break from it?** What do names in this space usually sound like, and does the product want to belong or stand apart?
5. **Hard constraints.** Must work as a verb, App Store name length, a competitor it cannot be confused with, a market where a word means something unfortunate.

Keep intake tight. Five questions, then generate.

## Two ways in

Generate names through these two paths, and group what you generate by which path produced it - so the designer is choosing between *approaches* first, then names within one.

### Primary path: job to real-world counterpart

This is the strongest method and the one to lead with. Name the function the product performs, then find a real-world role, object, or creature that performs that exact function. Then, if the product lives in a brand world, find the counterpart that lives in *that* world.

- A task-and-logistics coordinator, published by a pirate-themed company → the person who runs logistics on a ship is the *quartermaster*.
- A panel of stand-in customers you can test against on demand → the thing builders were missing was a *crew*.
- An app that lets you swipe someone's outfit and wear it yourself → raccoons are little bandits that swipe things → *Rackoon* (and the real word brings a free visual: a hanger, a masked face).

The reason this method works: a real-world counterpart that genuinely does the product's job makes the name *mean* something, and a real word drags its own image and often its own behavior along with it (raccoons swipe; a quartermaster coordinates). That gives the designer a visual anchor and a story for free.

### Secondary path: visual or logo first, name reverse-engineered

Sometimes the image comes before the word. A logo idea, an icon, a mark exists in the designer's head, and the name gets built to serve it. This is less common but legitimate - encode it so the skill recognizes and supports it rather than forcing job-first every time.

- The video icons - play, pause, stop - as the visual seed, then finding a short word those icons could spell → *Ovii*.

When a designer arrives with a visual already in mind, work backward from it: what words does this image support, spell, or evoke?

## The tests

Every candidate has to survive these. They are the standard, and they're weighted - the first two matter most because they're how good names actually earn their place.

1. **Does it do work?** Does the name say or evoke something true about the product's job? A name that means nothing about what the product does is starting from a deficit it has to pay down with marketing forever.

2. **Does it carry a visual?** Can you picture something when you hear it? Real words usually bring an image; coined words usually don't. When a name *does* have visual potential, surface the raw material for it - the referent, its associations, the forms it takes - and leave the seeing to the designer (see "What this skill does not do"). This test matters a lot: a name you can see is a name that can become a brand.

3. **Does it hold up as a verb?** Only if the product needs one - many do ("captain your projects," "borrow this look"). If the product's usage implies a verb, test whether the name survives becoming one.

4. **The wrong-image check.** When someone hears this name cold, do they picture the *wrong* thing? This is the failure that killed Fithaus - "a house you get fit in" landed as a gym, not a styling app. A name can do everything right on paper and still summon the wrong image in a stranger's head. (The skill can flag candidates at risk of this, but it can't run the test - see "Go test it" below.)

5. **The owned-term collision check.** Does the word already carry a strong, specific meaning in or near the product's space? This is a different failure than the wrong image. It killed Dock as a name: people already call the strip at the bottom of their Mac the Dock, so the name added confusion in the exact environment the product lived in, for no gain. A word that's already owned in your context costs clarity you don't get back.

6. **Can you build a story on it?** Does the name give you something to lean into - a world, an analogy, a through-line for the brand? Quartermaster opens up a whole ship's crew. Rackoon opens up the friendly-bandit angle. A name with story potential keeps paying off long after launch.

### On coined words versus real words

Stay neutral; surface the tradeoff. A real word doing double duty (Captain, Rackoon) brings a free visual and often a built-in behavior, and tends to score higher on tests 2 and 6. A coined word (Ovii) starts from zero on the visual and has to earn its meaning, but it owns its space cleanly, rarely collides with an owned term, and is usually easier to make available. Neither is better in the abstract. Name the tradeoff for the candidates in play and let the designer weigh it.

## Converge

Narrow to a shortlist of three to five, and for each one, say what it does well and what it trades off against the tests. Do not crown a winner. Light editorial guidance is welcome - "this one has the strongest visual but the weakest job-fit" - but the call stays with the designer, who has context the skill doesn't.

## Then, availability

Availability is a filter on the shortlist, not a gate at the top. Don't kill a strong name early because the exact domain is taken - shared names succeed constantly, and the right move is usually finding the gettable *form* of a name you already love.

- Get to a shortlist you'd be happy with first.
- Then check domains, App Store names, and handles for those.
- When the clean form is taken, look for the variation: a name became `yourcrew.app` rather than dying because `crew.com` wasn't available. The product prefix, the `.app`/`.co` variant, the "get"/"your" prefix - these are how a loved name gets a gettable form.
- Only treat availability as disqualifying when there's a genuine collision risk: a direct competitor, or a much larger company with the same name in an adjacent space.

## Go test it

There's one test the skill cannot run, and it's the most important one: saying the name out loud to real people and watching what image lands. Every name that's been killed for the right reason was killed this way - someone heard it and pictured the wrong thing, or pictured nothing.

So the skill's last move is to hand this back to the designer as an instruction, not to fake the result:

> Take your shortlist and say each name out loud to three or four people who'll be honest with you. Don't explain the product first. Ask them what they picture. Listen for the wrong picture - that's the name dying in real time, and it's the cheapest possible moment to find out.

## The shape of a naming pass

1. Confirm the brief back in one line (the job, the world, the feeling).
2. Names grouped by path - job-to-counterpart first, visual-first if relevant.
3. The shortlist of three to five, each with what it does well and what it trades off.
4. For any name with visual potential: the raw material for it, surfaced, not drawn.
5. Availability notes on the shortlist, with variations where the clean form is taken.
6. The "go test it" instruction.
7. Nothing else. No declared winner, no generated mark.
